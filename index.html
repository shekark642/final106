introduction

Here are the basics of what I am about to share with you. The dataset I analyzed consisted of hundreds of professional and amateur athletes, all doing a treadmill max performance test. Each participant would go through a warmup period on the treadmill, then slowly ramp up speed. Then after they were exhausted, they would slowly lower the speed as a cooldown period. Stats were measured throughout the test, and here is the analysis: First, have a slice!

Filtered Data Visualizations:

Lets start off with something simple. From what we can see here, we can make a few conclusions:

  • Average trend of speed and heart is to go up and down. This makes sense due to the nature of the treadmill test. People went fast and then slow.
  • The average time to tap out was around 800-900 seconds. 15 minutes of high intensity training was enough for these athletes, however, they were still able to do a cooldown, which means they were exhausted but not too exhausted
  • At the end, things got a little messy. This is a trend we will see a lot, but when you're heart rate is high and blood is pumping, people can be very different. As we can see, in the beginning, averages look pretty normal and clustered together, but later on, they fluctuate greatly. This is due to each person being different, however the scale of difference between the participants is seen much more towards the end of a run, especially as an older person.
The trends I will point out are common trends, however you may not see them as much depending on the filter you choose to apply Feel free to explore many different settings. I am here as an aid, to guide you through what you should be looking for, and what you may find, however, you may see different things as you explore different filters.

Now let's take a look at a different metric. This graph is about CO2 output and O2 input. The main thing we want to look for here is when the two lines intersect. We start with VO2 being on top and VCO2 being on the bottom. This makes sense as we are normally breathing more oxygen in than exhaling carbon dioxide. As the run progresses, you breathe more oxygen in and produce more carbon dioxide. This keeps going and soon you are producing more carbon dioxide than you can breathe in oxygen. This of course slows down after maximal effort. Comparing this graph about CO2 and O2 to the previous graph, we see some more interesting things:

  • The intersection period is very important, we can see that very shortly after this intersection period, the participant reaches exhaustion and starts cooling down. Producing more CO2 and breathing in O2 is a bad sign.
  • These two are very related. When one goes down, the other goes down in a very similar fashion, and vice versa.
  • VO2 and O2 are very stable metrics. Shortly after the run ends, these levels go back to normal.

Lets look at Pulmonary Ventilation, which is the movement of air into and out of the lungs. This is another standard metric that follows the trend of increasing during the max of the run and then goes down. This is a little bit unique because this metric is very peak oriented. VE increases up to a sharp peak and then linearly goes back down to normal levels

Gender Specific Info:

Apart from age, gender plays a huge role in athletic performace. Women and Men are physiologically very different, so its important to remember the differences.

Now Lets take a look at BMI. We will analyze VO2/HR and VCO2/HR data for 4 different participants of each group. This metric is important because it shows what a good amount of oxygen and carbon dioxide to have in your system. Of course, it is much healthier to have a lower BMI. There are exceptions like having a lot of muscle mass, which can raise your bmi and incorrectly determine you are unhealthy. What can be seen is that the y values for lower bmi athletes are lower. They do not need as much O2 to run, and produce less CO2. These 2 values are more muted as they do not fluctuate in the y direction is much, but the overall y value is very imporant as well.
